Success Criteria & Quality Bar

Every prompt you submit must meet all of these criteria to pass QA and be accepted into the dataset. These rules define what “good enough” looks like for this project.

Complexity & Difficulty

  • Contains at least four genuine reasoning layers (four distinct clues that are needed to reach the answer, not filler details).
  • Requires at least four separate web sources to fully verify the reasoning path.
  • Would take most experts several minutes to solve and reverse‑engineer, not something trivial or direct.
  • Is not solved by a simple search of the full question text (the answer should not pop out from a single, obvious query).

Answer Properties

  • The answer is a single, unambiguous ground‑truth string (no multiple valid answers, no comma‑separated lists).
  • The answer is string‑matchable: the correct model response should contain the exact ground‑truth substring regardless of capitalization.
  • The answer is evergreen: it will remain the same over time and does not depend on current events or changing facts.
  • The answer is not easily guessable from the prompt alone (e.g., not a very common or obvious choice).

Source & Formatting Rules

  • Uses multiple independent sources; no more than half of the sources for a prompt may be from Wikipedia.
  • All sources used in the rubric are English‑language web pages that can be accessed without extra clicks (no logins, pagination, or interactive dashboards).
  • All prompts and citations are text‑only; do not rely on images, audio, or video.
  • Numeric answers follow the required format exactly: no commas unless explicitly specified (e.g., 491 or 491k in the pattern “xxxk”).

Style & Professionalism

  • Prompt and answer are written in clear, grammatical English, with professional tone and no run‑on sentences.
  • Prompts across your batch are diverse in topic and structure; do not recycle the same pattern repeatedly.
  • No counting‑based tricks (e.g., “third letter of the word,” “fourth letter of the song title,” initials puzzles, etc.).
  • Prompts are non‑instructional: they do not walk the model through a step‑by‑step search plan.
